# Bobby's story

A bit of how I got here, in my own words, in case you want the story behind the resume.

## How I got into data

My first real working experience was at First Code Academy back in 2017, teaching kids how
to code across a bunch of languages, from block-based stuff like Scratch all the way to Unity
and Python. That job taught me something I still lean on every day: how to take a technical
concept and break it into digestible blocks that even a kid can follow. That skill carried me
far in corporate, because so much of the work is turning something technical into something a
non-technical stakeholder can actually understand.

After that I did a short stint at ISS Facility Services as a data analyst. Honestly it was an
old-school corporate job and they weren't quite sure what to do with me. The one thing I'm
proud of from there was taking their raw Excel data and building a proper dashboard out of it.
Beyond that I mostly spent my time self-learning, so within two years I moved on.

Then came Shopee, where I've been for close to six years now. This is where I built my real
toolkit: SQL, ETLs, dashboarding, ad-hoc analyses, A/B testing, stakeholder management, and
the harder skill of turning an ambiguous question into a real, data-ready one. A big part of
the job is just being solid data support that people can rely on.

## Getting into AI

The bigger shift happened from around mid 2025 to now. That's when I got deep into the AI
space, because it became really clear how much it improves productivity when you use it
properly. I decided this is where I want to keep growing, using tools like Claude Code and
Cursor to build actual analytical solutions: LLM data annotation, chatbots, that kind of thing.

I feel strongly that any analyst who doesn't embrace AI is going to get left behind by the
ones who genuinely know how to use it. And I mean genuinely, not the people just churning out
slop. Used right, AI is a brilliant tool that can solve big problems in a short amount of time.

## What hooked me

It was really the chatbot and data-tagging solutions I built. When I looked at the responses
the chatbot was generating, I could tell it wasn't something a human could produce that fast.
Sure, sometimes it looks obviously AI-generated, but the core information is all there and it's
thorough, and with a bit of time and proper customization the slop keeps reducing.

Honestly, without AI I probably wouldn't have built this website at all, let alone wired up a
chatbot with Supabase in about a week. That's the part that excites me, taking something that
would have been a big project and making it real, quickly.

## How I work

When someone hands me a vague one-liner request, my instinct is to grill it with clarifying
questions before I touch anything (I even lean on a Claude skill for exactly this). On the
surface it can look like I'm creating friction instead of just getting the work done, but
really I'm trying to understand the requirement in as much detail as possible and document it
as I go, so it's easier to communicate later when everything's written down.

If someone isn't so keen on the back-and-forth, I'll just pull them into a meeting and
whiteboard it until we align quickly. And a lot of the time I don't wait to be asked. I like
to listen to how people actually work, spot the common problems, and proactively build
something for them. That's how Boko came about, a self-serve bot that explains metric logic to
end users, along with a bunch of Claude skills I built to make Clyde more useful.

## What I've learned the hard way

The biggest lesson: shipping too fast can actually reduce visibility. When I move quickly with
AI, it can look like low effort from the outside, when really I just know how to leverage the
tools to get there faster. What fixed this for me was involving stakeholders during the build
instead of after. People appreciate the final solution a lot more when they helped shape it,
versus me building it in a corner and then selling it to them like I'm the one who's right.

The customer is always right. Always collaborate to build the solution they actually want, not
the one you assume they want.

## What I want next

Genuinely, I want to build more automation for end users. It doesn't have to be AI automation,
it could be anything that solves an immediate problem, especially for small and medium
businesses. And if I'm working within a company, roles like AI product manager, AI engineer,
or AI analytics are where I'm most excited to grow.
